Budgeting Lessons

Learn the fundamentals of budgeting for agricultural and business settings through these interactive lessons.

Lesson 1: Introduction to Budgeting

A budget is a plan that helps you keep track of your money. It shows how much money you expect to get (income) and how much you plan to spend (expenses) over a certain time period.

Why Budgeting Matters in Agriculture

  • Plan for seasonal changes in income
  • Prepare for unexpected events like weather problems
  • Make smart decisions about buying equipment
  • Know when you can afford to grow your business

Key Budget Terms

  • Income: Money you receive from selling products or services
  • Expenses: Money you spend to run your business
  • Profit: Money left over after paying all expenses
  • Cash Flow: The movement of money in and out of your business

Lesson 2: Setting Up a Budget

Creating a budget involves identifying all sources of income and all expenses for your farm or business.

Steps to Create a Budget:

  1. Choose a time period (monthly, quarterly, or yearly)
  2. List all sources of income
  3. List all expenses
  4. Calculate total income and total expenses
  5. Calculate profit or loss (income minus expenses)

Common Farm Income Categories:

  • Crop sales (wheat, corn, vegetables, etc.)
  • Livestock sales (cattle, sheep, poultry, etc.)
  • Dairy products
  • Government payments or subsidies

Common Farm Expense Categories:

  • Seeds and plants
  • Fertilizer and chemicals
  • Animal feed
  • Veterinary services
  • Fuel and utilities
  • Equipment repairs and maintenance
  • Labor/wages
  • Insurance
  • Loan payments

Lesson 3: Tracking Actuals vs. Budget

Once you have a budget, you need to track your actual income and expenses to see how they compare to your budget.

Why Track Actuals:

  • See if your budget is realistic
  • Identify areas where you're spending more or less than planned
  • Make adjustments to stay on track
  • Plan better for the future

Variance Analysis:

A variance is the difference between your budgeted amount and the actual amount.

  • Positive variance: Better than budget (higher income or lower expenses)
  • Negative variance: Worse than budget (lower income or higher expenses)

To calculate a variance:

  • For income: Actual - Budget
  • For expenses: Budget - Actual

This way, a positive number always means a favorable variance.

Lesson 4: Making Budget Adjustments

When your actual figures differ from your budget, you may need to make adjustments to your budget or your operations.

Types of Adjustments:

  1. Revise the budget: Update your budget to reflect the new reality
  2. Change operations: Adjust your activities to get back on budget
  3. Reallocate resources: Move money from one category to another

When to Make Adjustments:

  • When variances are significant (more than 10%)
  • When variances are persistent (happening month after month)
  • When external factors change (weather, market prices, etc.)

Examples of Budget Adjustments:

  • If feed costs are higher than budgeted, you might look for cheaper suppliers or adjust your livestock numbers
  • If crop sales are lower than budgeted, you might need to find new markets or adjust your planting plans
  • If equipment repairs are higher than budgeted, you might need to consider replacing old equipment
